History Introduction

Unfolding the History of Origami

Early Beginnings and Invention of Origami

It is quite difficult to trace the exact origins and plot a comprehensive and concise timeline of origami’s history. However, despite most of its information being disintegrated, it is believed that Japan’s tradition of paper folding began shortly after Buddhist monks from China brought paper to Korea and Japan at around the sixth (6th) Century. First appearing in China around the 2nd century BCE, paper replaced heavier writing materials such as bamboo and wood.

Integration of Origami into Japanese Culture

Shortly thereafter, the art of folding paper was limited only to religious and ceremonial use as paper was expensive. Girei-ori, or ceremonial folds, were done by the Ogasawara and Ise clans- noble families who developed this strict folding style for ceremonies. It was until various Japanese feudal lords, during the Edo Period (1603-1868), encouraged the expansion of local paper making industries that led to the mass production of paper and inspirational recreation in the form of orisue (meaning “folded setting down”) or origata (meaning “folded shape”), currently known as origami.


With paper now being affordable, and origami spreading amongst the masses, paper folding flourished in Japan. Noshi is known as the earliest non-religious use of folded paper, wherein a sort of folded chevron was given with a gift as a token of good fortune, as well as tsutsumi- formal gift wrapping. During Shinto weddings, paired butterflies adorned sake bottles as the first representatives of origami. Additionally, cranes and samurai dolls were folded into reality and were integrated into Japan’s rich culture.


In addition, in 1797, the oldest known origami book was publish- known as Hiden Senbazuru Orikata (Secret Folding Methods of a Thousands Cranes). Demonstrating basic diagrammatic instructions on 49 different forms of paper cranes, the love for paper folding was clearly emphasized in Japan; with origami even entering curriculums during the Meiji period- allowing children to learn patience, balance, and precision.

Global Influence and Origami Spreading Worldwide

The tradition of paper folding had also independently developed in Europe in the 11th or 12th century in Spain and Germany, with the early 1800s displaying folded paper animals, horses and riders in German museum collections. Friedrich Frobel, a German Educator who created the first kindergarten, used paper folding as well as creative lessons to teach children. European folding patterns also allowed teachers to introduce geometry to kindergarteners since it tended to be more geometric and used 45-degree folds, rather than Japanese 22.5-degree folds.


During the late 19th to mid-20th century, orisue or origata developed from its original root–oru (to fold)–and soon settled to origami (meaning “folded paper”). Born in 1911 in Kaminokawa Tochigi, Akira Yoshizawa is currently considered the father of origami. Folding an estimate of more than 50,000 models in his entire lifetime, he also published Atarashi Origami Heijutsu (New Origami Art) in 1954, laying the foundation for what is now known as the Yoshizawa-Randlett system of notation of origami folds- using mountain and valley folds represented by dashed and dotted lines.


Concurrently, figures resembling cranes, frogs and flowers are folded by common people with chiyogami- a single sheet of paper without requiring glue or scissors. Other techniques that include cutting and using glue have also been developed however. Modular origami is another origami technique that uses multiple folded shapes to form larger constructions, wet folding allows creations to have more curved shapes, and action origami allows origami to move such as jumping frogs.

Modern Applications inspiring Architecture and Robotics

While origami is usually seen on tables or displayed as art, it also has many modern applications in Science. According to a theoretical study titled “Origami-Inspired Living Panels For Energy-efficient And Power-Generating Buildings” conducted in the Rochester Institute of Technology (2024, Basta), origami principles could be applied to solar panels. This would optimize effective capturing and conversion of solar energy, in light of incentives to reduce energy consumption and to mitigate the adverse effects of climate change and greenhouse gas emissions. Aiming to maximize solar energy capture, enhance thermal management, and generate renewable energy, origami techniques were applied to reflective panels that could direct sunlight to specific areas such as photovoltaic cells (panels that convert sunlight directly into electricity) or thermal collectors. This maximized reflection and collection of sunlight during peak hours, increasing energy captured by the system. Concentrating sunlight into smaller, highly efficient areas can also increase thermal energy available for conversion.

Origami also skyrocketed and is used in space exploration. Inspired by origami, NASA’s golden starshade prototype is a large, foldable structure designed to block starlight, allowing telescopes to capture clearer images of distant exoplanets.
